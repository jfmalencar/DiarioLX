\chapter{Data Model} \label{cap:datamodel}

This chapter describes the data model that underpins \textit{Diário LX}. It begins with
the \emph{conceptual} model (Section~\ref{sec:dm_conceptual}) --- the entities of the domain,
their attributes, and the relationships between them --- and then explains how that model was
normalised to Third Normal Form and mapped onto the physical PostgreSQL schema
(Section~\ref{sec:dm_physical}). Since the complete entity-relationship diagram spans the
entire domain and does not fit legibly within the text, it is provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:er_full} (Figure~\ref{fig:er_full}); the reader may find it helpful to
consult that diagram alongside the descriptions below. The full physical schema --- every
table, column, enumerated type, view, and index --- is given in
Appendix~\ref{app:physical_schema}.

\section{Conceptual Model} \label{sec:dm_conceptual}

At the conceptual level the model centres on the \emph{content} item, which represents any
publication --- an article, video, podcast, episode, or photo essay. A content item
belongs to a \emph{category} (which may itself have a parent category, forming
subcategories), is written by one or more \emph{users} acting as its authors, and is
labelled with one or more \emph{tags}. Its body is an ordered sequence of \emph{blocks},
some of which reference \emph{media} items, and a podcast \emph{episode} is a content item
attached to a parent podcast. Every moderation decision on a content item is recorded as a
\emph{history} entry attributed to the acting user, and editorial staff curate the homepage
through \emph{featured sections}, each holding an ordered selection of content items. Around
this editorial core sit the entities that govern access: \emph{users} authenticate through
\emph{sessions}, are onboarded through \emph{invites}, and recover access through
\emph{password reset requests}. Site-wide configuration is held in a generic \emph{settings}
store.

The entities are described below, grouped by concern; each group is accompanied by a diagram
of its entities and their attributes. The diagrams use Chen's entity-relationship
notation~\cite{chen1976er}, in which entities are drawn as rectangles, relationships as
diamonds, and attributes as ellipses, with doubled borders marking weak entities and their
identifying relationships. The relationships between these entities are
not repeated in those diagrams but are shown together in the complete entity-relationship
diagram in Appendix~\ref{app:er_full} (Figure~\ref{fig:er_full}); the only
exception is the
attribute-less \emph{Navigation} entity, whose relationship to \emph{Category} is shown in its
group diagram (Figure~\ref{fig:dm_featured}), as it has no attributes of its own to depict.
Where an attribute is restricted to a fixed set of values, that domain is captured by an
enumerated type; these are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:dm_enums}.

\subsection{Users, Sessions, Invites, and Password Resets} \label{sec:dm_identity}

\begin{figure}[H]
      \begin{center}
            \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{./figures/er/er_users.png}}
      \end{center}
      \caption{Identity and access entities and their attributes.}
      \label{fig:dm_identity}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:dm_identity} presents these entities and their attributes.
A \textbf{User} represents an authenticated member of the editorial team. Each user has a
role (\textit{ADMIN}, \textit{EDITOR}, or \textit{CONTRIBUTOR}), credentials
(\texttt{username}, \texttt{email}, and a hashed password), and profile information
(\texttt{first\_name}, \texttt{last\_name}, \texttt{position}, \texttt{bio}, and an optional
avatar). An \texttt{on\_team} flag marks users that appear on the public team page, and an
\texttt{active\_account} flag allows an account to be deactivated without deleting its
record.

A \textbf{Session} records an active login. It holds the refresh token issued at
authentication together with its creation and expiration instants, and belongs to exactly
one user; sessions are removed on logout and expire automatically.

An \textbf{Invite} is a single-use token generated by an administrator to allow a new
account to register. It carries the token itself, the role the future account will receive,
an expiration instant, and a reference to the administrator that created it. An invite is
consumed --- and removed --- the moment it is used, so a code can never be reused.

A \textbf{Password Reset Request} represents a user's request to recover access. It records
the requesting user, a status, the administrator that approves it, a single-use reset token,
and the relevant timestamps.

\subsection{Content} \label{sec:dm_content}

\begin{figure}[H]
      \begin{center}
            \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{./figures/er/er_contents.png}}
      \end{center}
      \caption{The content item and the entities that make up its body.}
      \label{fig:dm_content}
\end{figure}

The content item and the entities that make up its body are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:dm_content}. The \textbf{Content} item is the central entity of the system. Each item has a
\texttt{type}, a \texttt{title}, a \texttt{headline}, and a \texttt{slug}, belongs to a
category, and progresses through an editorial \texttt{state} (\textit{DRAFT},
\textit{PENDING\_REVIEW}, \textit{APPROVED}, or \textit{REJECTED}). Approval and publication
are modelled as distinct concerns: the state records the editorial decision, while a
\texttt{published\_at} instant governs when the item actually becomes visible --- an item is
shown to readers only once it is \textit{APPROVED} and that instant has passed, which also
allows publication to be scheduled ahead of time. A podcast episode references its parent
podcast through a self-relationship, and video or episode items may point to an external
embed (a YouTube video or a Spotify episode) instead of an uploaded file. Items can be
soft-archived rather than deleted.

A \textbf{Content Block} is a single ordered unit of a content item's body. Each block has a
\texttt{type} (a paragraph, quotation, heading, embed, single media item, or gallery) and a
\texttt{position} that fixes its order within the item; depending on its type it either
holds text or references a media item.

\textbf{Content Block Images} hold the ordered set of images that make up a
\textit{gallery} block. Each records the referenced media item, an optional \texttt{caption},
and its \texttt{position} within the gallery.

\subsection{Authors, Tags, and Moderation History} \label{sec:dm_editorial}

\begin{figure}[H]
      \begin{center}
            \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{./figures/er/er_content_related.png}}
      \end{center}
      \caption{Entities associating a content item with its authors, tags, and review trail.}
      \label{fig:dm_editorial}
\end{figure}

These three entities are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dm_editorial}.
\textbf{Content Authors} associates a content item with the users that wrote it, each with a
\texttt{role} of \textit{primary} or \textit{secondary}; a content item has at most one
primary author. \textbf{Content Tags} likewise associates a content item with its tags,
again distinguishing a single primary tag from the secondary ones.

\textbf{Content History} records the moderation trail of a content item. Each entry captures
the action taken (\textit{APPROVED} or \textit{REJECTED}), an optional review comment, the
instant it was performed, and the user who performed it. This provides an auditable log of
the approval workflow and supplies the mandatory justification attached to a rejection.

\subsection{Categories and Tags} \label{sec:dm_taxonomy}

\begin{figure}[H]
      \begin{center}
            \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{./figures/er/er_categories_slugs.png}}
      \end{center}
      \caption{The taxonomy entities.}
      \label{fig:dm_taxonomy}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:dm_taxonomy} shows both taxonomy entities.
A \textbf{Category} organises content editorially and supports a two-level hierarchy through
a self-relationship: top-level categories have no parent, and subcategories reference their
parent. Each category has a \texttt{name}, a unique \texttt{slug}, a distinctive
\texttt{colour} used throughout the interface, and an optional description, and may be
soft-archived.

A \textbf{Tag} provides cross-cutting thematic classification independent of the category
hierarchy. Each tag has a \texttt{name}, a unique \texttt{slug}, and an optional
description, and may also be soft-archived. A single content item may carry several tags.

\subsection{Media} \label{sec:dm_media}

\begin{figure}[H]
      \begin{center}
            \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{./figures/er/er_medias.png}}
      \end{center}
      \caption{The media entity and its credits.}
      \label{fig:dm_media}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:dm_media} shows the media entity and its credits.
A \textbf{Media} item stores the metadata of an uploaded file --- image, video, or audio ---
whose binary content is held externally rather than in the database. It records the media
\texttt{purpose}, its storage location, alternative text, MIME type, size, and an upload
\texttt{status} that moves from \textit{pending} to \textit{ready} once the upload is
confirmed.

\textbf{Media Credits} attribute a media item to the users that contributed to it, each with
a \texttt{credit\_role} (such as \textit{PHOTOGRAPHER} or \textit{VIDEOGRAPHER}), so that a
single person may hold more than one role on the same item.

\subsection{Homepage Curation and Settings} \label{sec:dm_featured}

\begin{figure}[H]
      \begin{center}
            \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\includegraphics{./figures/er/er_settings.png}}
      \end{center}
      \caption{The homepage-curation and configuration entities.}
      \label{fig:dm_featured}
\end{figure}

The homepage and public navigation are curated through a few related entities, shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:dm_featured}. A \textbf{Featured Section} defines an ordered section of the
homepage and has a \texttt{type} and a \texttt{position}; category sections additionally
reference the category they display. \textbf{Featured Contents} then place individual
content items within a section, each with a \texttt{position} that fixes its order. The
public navigation bar is modelled by a \textbf{Navigation} entity, which has no attributes of
its own; the categories highlighted there are given by a relationship between \emph{Category}
and \emph{Navigation} --- ``a category can appear in the navigation'' --- that carries a
single relationship attribute, \texttt{position}, fixing the order in which those categories
are shown.

Finally, \textbf{Settings}\label{sec:dm_settings} is a generic key/value store for the
remaining site-wide configuration --- social-media links, contact information, and the
navigation toggles that control whether the photos, podcasts, and videos sections are shown
--- allowing these values to be edited by administrators without schema changes.

\section{Normalisation and Physical Model} \label{sec:dm_physical}

The conceptual model was translated into a relational schema and normalised to Third Normal
Form (3NF)~\cite{codd1972normalization, elmasri2016fundamentals}, the standard target for a
transactional relational database. Informally, 3NF requires that every non-key attribute
depend on the key, the whole key, and nothing but the key. In practice this meant, in
increasing order of the normal forms: storing only atomic values and moving every repeating
group --- a content item's authors, tags, and blocks, or a media item's credits --- into its
own table rather than into arrays or delimited strings (First Normal Form); ensuring that,
in tables with a composite key, no attribute depends on only part of that key (Second Normal
Form); and removing transitive dependencies, so that derived or dependent data is not
duplicated across tables (Third Normal Form).

The mapping from the conceptual model to the physical schema follows the usual relational
transformation rules:

\begin{itemize}
      \item each \textbf{entity} becomes a table with an auto-incrementing \texttt{SERIAL}
            surrogate primary key;
      \item each \textbf{one-to-many relationship} becomes a foreign key on the table at the
            ``many'' end (for example, a content item's category, or a session's user).
            Where the relationship is optional and carries its own attributes,
            a separate table may be used instead. For example,
            \texttt{navigation\_featured\_categories} implements the
            \emph{Category}--\emph{Navigation} relationship by storing the
            \texttt{position} of each category within a navigation section while avoiding a
            nullable foreign key in \texttt{categories};
      \item each \textbf{many-to-many relationship} becomes an \emph{association table}
            keyed by the foreign keys it relates and carrying any attributes of the
            relationship: \texttt{content\_authors}, \texttt{content\_tags}, and
            \texttt{media\_credits} record a \texttt{role}, while
            \texttt{featured\_contents} records the \texttt{position} of a content item
            within a homepage section;
      \item \textbf{weak entities}, whose identity depends on a parent --- the images of a
            gallery block (\texttt{content\_block\_images}, keyed by block and position) ---
            take a composite primary key drawn from that parent;
      \item \textbf{hierarchies and self-references} --- a category's parent, and a
            podcast's episodes --- are expressed as a foreign key from a table to itself.
\end{itemize}

Two conventions apply schema-wide. All timestamps are stored as \texttt{BIGINT} Unix-epoch
values --- inherently UTC, which sidesteps timezone conversion and maps directly onto the
\texttt{kotlinx.datetime.Instant} type used in the backend --- and every table uses a
compact surrogate key rather than a UUID, keeping joins and indexes small for the platform's
expected data volume. Several attribute domains are further constrained by PostgreSQL
enumerated types (Appendix~\ref{app:dm_enums}), and referential integrity is enforced with
foreign-key constraints whose deletion behaviour reflects each relationship --- cascading
where a child cannot outlive its parent (a content item's blocks), or nulling the reference
where it can (a category's subcategories when the parent is removed).

Built on top of this schema, a set of PostgreSQL \emph{views} encapsulates the recurring
join and aggregation logic used by the repository layer, and a set of \emph{indexes}
supports the most frequent query patterns; two partial unique indexes additionally enforce
the ``at most one primary author'' and ``at most one primary tag'' rules that the
association tables alone cannot express. The complete physical schema --- all tables,
enumerated types, views, and indexes --- is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:physical_schema}.
